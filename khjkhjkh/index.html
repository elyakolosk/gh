Why cheap bags are more expensive than you think

Elvira Mera June 11, 2026
Elvira, we're too poor to buy cheap things.

My mother used to say this all the time when I was growing up. We were genuinely poor. My mother earned less than $200 a month, and my father was an alcoholic who eventually disappeared from our lives. Every purchase mattered. We didn't have much room for mistakes, so the idea of spending more on anything sounded ridiculous. Surely if you don't have money, you buy the cheapest option available. That seemed logical to me.

It took me years to understand what she actually meant. What she was really saying was that a cheap thing is only cheap on the day you buy it. If it breaks after a few months, if it falls apart, if it needs replacing, then suddenly it wasn't a bargain at all. In many cases, you've spent more money than if you had bought something better in the first place. My mother wasn't talking about fashion when she said it. She wasn't talking about bags either. She was talking about value.

Why this lesson followed me into adulthood

The older I get, the more often I find myself coming back to that idea. We live in a world that constantly encourages us to buy MORE. Every week there is a new trend, a new must-have item, a new reason why the things we already own suddenly seem inadequate. I've noticed that many people focus almost entirely on the purchase price. They ask whether something is cheap or expensive, but rarely ask whether it's actually worth the money. Those are very different questions. Some expensive things are terrible value. Some affordable things are excellent value. Learning the difference is probably one of the most useful financial skills anyone can develop.

Why bags became my thing

People often ask why I'm so interested in bags. Honestly, I don't have a particularly deep or romantic answer. It just happened. I've never been especially interested in clothing. A few years ago I challenged myself not to buy any new clothes for an entire year. I expected it to be difficult, but it wasn't. I captured this challenge on my personal TikTok here.

Bags were a different story. For some reason they've always fascinated me. I notice details that most people probably ignore. The leather. The suede. The stitching. The proportions. The hardware. The way a bag ages after years of use. The difference between a bag that looks good in a product photo and a bag that actually works in real life. 

Most people don't have a bag problem

One thing I've realised over the years is that most people don't actually have a bag problem. They have a shopping problem. We're constantly told that we need something new. A new colour. A new shape. A new trend. A new version of something we already own. But when I look at the bags I've loved the most over the years, none of them were exciting trend purchases. They were the bags that quietly became part of my everyday life. The ones that worked. The ones I reached for without thinking. The ones that survived daily use and still looked good years later. That's why I'm much more interested in quality, practicality, and longevity than in whatever trend happens to be popular this season.

Why I started this blog

This blog is about bags, but it's also about the decisions behind them. I live in Italy and have spent years working around fashion, retail, and consumer products. 

My fascination with bags eventually led me much further. During my master's degree in Fashion Culture and Management in Italy, they became one of the inspirations behind my dissertation on counterfeit fashion products. The more I looked at bags, the more questions I had about value, branding, authenticity, and why people choose one product over another. What started as an interest in bags eventually became an interest in consumer behaviour itself.

Over the years I've looked at thousands of bags. I've seen trends come and go. I've seen prices rise. I've seen quality improve in some places and disappear completely in others. I've seen people spend far too much money on things they didn't need and ignore things that would have served them well for years. Eventually, I realised I had a lot of thoughts about all of this. This blog is where I'll share them.

What You'll Find On This Blog

This isn't a luxury blog, and it isn't a fashion magazine. I'm not interested in convincing anyone to buy more things.In fact, I think most people already own more than enough.  Why some stay in our wardrobes for years while others lose their appeal after a few months. Why some bags justify their price, and why others don't. Here you'll find honest thoughts about bag quality, leather and suede, craftsmanship, value for money, trends, and the strange ways fashion encourages us to consume more than we need. I'll share reviews, comparisons, observations from everyday life in Italy, and lessons I've learned after spending years working around fashion, retail, and consumer products.

Most of all, this blog is about buying better, not buying more.
